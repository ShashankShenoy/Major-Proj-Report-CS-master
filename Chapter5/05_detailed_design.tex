\chapter{Detailed Design of Maritime Surveillance and Trajectory Prediction}

This chapter delves into the detailed design of the system's functional modules. It covers the hierarchical structure chart, algorithmic selection for detection and prediction, dataset analysis and preprocessing, and the data fusion processing pipeline necessary for real-time operation.

\section{Structure Chart}


The proposed maritime surveillance framework is organized as a hierarchical collection of functional modules, each responsible for a specific stage of the surveillance workflow. By distinctly separating the perception engines (such as YOLOv8) from the state estimation protocols (like Kalman Filtering and DeepOCSORT), the architecture effectively prevents computationally heavy tasks from creating downstream bottlenecks. This strict compartmentalization also ensures that if one module fails or encounters corrupted data, the disruption does not cascade through the entire network.

At the highest level of this hierarchy, the system is bifurcated into two parallel ingestion pipelines: one dedicated to raw optical video streams and another handling automated identification system telemetry. This dual-pipeline approach guarantees that visual data, which requires intensive graphical processing unit acceleration, does not stall the lightweight, asynchronous parsing of navigational metadata. By decoupling these inputs at the architectural root, the system can dynamically adjust its resource allocation based on the density of maritime traffic within the camera's field of view.

Beneath the ingestion layer lies the core analytical engine, which orchestrates the complex interaction between detection, tracking, and prediction models. Rather than relying on a monolithic software design, this engine utilizes a microservices-inspired architecture. Data structures representing vessel bounding boxes and unique identifiers are passed sequentially between discrete functions. This design explicitly localizes state management; the tracking module maintains its own history of object trajectories independently from the detection module's instantaneous frame analysis. Consequently, memory leaks or overflow errors are strictly confined to their respective domains.

Finally, the presentation layer sits at the top of the structural hierarchy, acting as the sole interface for the end-user. It aggregates the disparate outputs of the analytical engine—combining real-time video, predicted geospatial arcs, and collision alerts—into a unified dashboard. Because the rendering thread is physically separated from the background deep learning inference processes, the dashboard remains highly responsive even during periods of extreme computational load. This hierarchical design not only streamlines the immediate processing workflow but also simplifies future debugging and maintenance efforts.

\subsection{System Structure and Micro-Architecture}
To ensure robust real-time performance, the system is engineered to strict technical parameters. It requires a continuous video feed with a minimum resolution of $1280 \times 720$ pixels, targeting a processing throughput of 30 frames per second. The underlying \gls{yolov8} detection module operates with a confidence threshold of 0.4 and utilizes an \gls{iou} threshold of 0.3 for \gls{nms}, which balances sensitivity against the suppression of false positives. Downstream, the \gls{deepocsort} tracker uses an \gls{osnet} backbone with a cosine similarity metric for appearance-based \gls{reid}. To prevent the proliferation of ghost tracks, the tracker is configured with a maximum track age of 30 frames, a minimum confirmation hit count of 3, and a detection threshold of 0.3.

These highly specific parameters were not chosen arbitrarily; they are the result of extensive empirical testing across various simulated maritime environments. For instance, setting the \gls{nms} \gls{iou} threshold strictly to 0.3 ensures that closely passing vessels are not erroneously merged into a single bounding box by the detector, a frequent issue in congested ports. Similarly, the 30-frame track age limit provides the tracker with just enough memory to survive a temporary occlusion—such as a small boat passing behind a larger cargo ship—without unnecessarily draining system resources by tracking objects that have permanently left the camera's field of view. The comprehensive parameter matrix is outlined in Table~\ref{tab:design_specs}.

In determining these parameters, a rigorous balance had to be struck between computational efficiency and detection accuracy. High-resolution input streams inherently provide greater pixel density for distant targets, significantly improving the recall of small vessels on the horizon. However, processing native 4K video feeds through complex convolutional neural networks would drastically reduce the frames-per-second throughput, introducing unacceptable latency into the alerting pipeline. By standardizing the input to a $1280 \times 720$ resolution, the system maintains sufficient spatial detail to identify far-off ships while ensuring the YOLOv8 tensor operations complete within the strict millisecond constraints required for real-time tracking.

Furthermore, the confidence threshold of 0.4 was selected after analyzing the receiver operating characteristic curves during the validation phase. Lowering this threshold resulted in an influx of false positives—frequently misclassifying wave crests, buoys, or low-flying seagulls as watercraft. Conversely, raising the threshold above 0.5 led to missed detections under poor lighting conditions, such as dusk or heavy fog. The 0.4 setpoint acts as the optimal mathematical fulcrum, ensuring high sensitivity to legitimate threats without overwhelming the tracker with noise.

Additionally, the physical hardware environment plays a critical role in supporting these specifications. The entire pipeline is heavily reliant on \gls{nvidia} \gls{cuda} acceleration, taking advantage of parallelized GPU architectures to process the intense matrix multiplications demanded by both the YOLOv8 and \gls{bilstm} networks. Without hardware-level acceleration, the stated 30 FPS target would be unattainable, and the chronological synchronization between the rapid visual tracking updates and the slower \gls{ais} data ingestion would collapse. These meticulously tuned parameters thus reflect the physical constraints of the deployment hardware just as much as the mathematical requirements of the deep learning algorithms.

For the predictive modeling phase, the \gls{lstm} module processes a historical sequence of 50 timesteps to forecast the subsequent 20 positions of each vessel. During geospatial mapping, pixel coordinates are mapped to geographic latitude and longitude via a homographic transformation matrix (computed via \texttt{cv2.findHomography} using ground-control points). When such points are unavailable, an \gls{fov} linear approximation serves as a reliable fallback. The derived \gls{gps} positions are smoothed using a per-vessel constant-velocity Kalman filter to mitigate detection jitter. Finally, to ensure physical validity, all forecasted trajectories are validated against a geographic water-area filter. The entire pipeline is implemented in Python, leveraging \gls{pytorch} for deep learning and \gls{opencv} for frame processing, with full support for \gls{nvidia} \gls{cuda} hardware acceleration.

\subsection{Use Case Analysis}
By analyzing these user flows, it becomes clear that the system is built to function with minimal manual intervention. The primary actor, the port operator, is largely insulated from the complex mathematical processing occurring in the backend. Instead of having to manually correlate radar pings with video feeds, the operator simply monitors the synthesized dashboard. This design drastically reduces the cognitive load on security personnel, allowing them to shift their focus from raw data interpretation to high-level decision-making and emergency response. 

To fully appreciate this reduction in cognitive load, it is essential to examine the specific use cases the system natively supports. Under normal operating conditions, the primary use case is passive monitoring. The system continuously ingests video and \gls{ais} data without requiring any prompting from the operator. During this state, the background services autonomously manage the extraction of vessel features, the assignment of tracking IDs, and the real-time geographic projection. The operator's interaction is limited to observing the unified dashboard, where all relevant data is preemptively visualized.

A secondary, more active use case occurs when the system identifies a potential anomaly, such as a 'dark vessel' that lacks a corresponding \gls{ais} signal, or when the trajectory module predicts an imminent collision based on the Closest Point of Approach (CPA). In these scenarios, the system shifts from passive monitoring to active alerting. The dashboard immediately flags the suspicious or endangered vessels, drawing the operator's attention directly to the spatial coordinates of the event. The operator can then interact with the alert, drilling down into the historical trajectory data or the raw camera feed to verify the threat.

Finally, the architecture accounts for edge cases involving sensor failure or extreme weather. If the video feed degrades due to heavy fog, the system automatically falls back to relying more heavily on \gls{ais} interpolation for known targets. Conversely, if \gls{ais} telemetry drops out due to signal spoofing or hardware failure, the vision-based tracking module sustains the vessel's trajectory using purely optical prediction. The operator is notified of these sensor degradation events through system health indicators on the dashboard, ensuring complete transparency regarding the platform's operational status at any given moment. These carefully defined use cases guarantee that human intervention is reserved exclusively for critical decision-making, rather than routine data management.

\subsection{Data Schemas, Spatial Transformation, and Preprocessing}
The system relies on two specialized datasets: one for fine-tuning the \gls{yolov8} detector, and another for training the \gls{lstm} predictive model. Both were carefully selected based on their environmental diversity, annotation quality, and relevance to maritime scenarios.

For vessel detection, the \textit{Kaggle Ships in Google Earth} dataset (sourced via Roboflow) was utilized. This dataset provides a vast collection of aerial and satellite imagery annotated with \gls{yolo}-formatted bounding boxes. Its diversity in sea states, illumination, and occlusion scenarios makes it highly representative of real-world surveillance environments. The YOLOv8l variant was fine-tuned on this dataset for 70 epochs at a $640 \times 640$ resolution.

Training on such a diverse corpus ensures that the convolutional layers of the network learn to identify the intrinsic structural features of maritime vessels, rather than merely memorizing the color of the water or the specific lighting conditions of the training images. This is particularly crucial for maintaining high recall rates during adverse weather events, such as torrential rain or heavy fog, where traditional edge-detection algorithms frequently fail. Furthermore, the inclusion of images taken from varying altitudes forces the network to become scale-invariant, allowing it to detect a massive freighter miles away just as easily as a small tugboat operating directly beneath the camera.

To further enhance this generalization capability, an extensive data augmentation pipeline was applied prior to training. Techniques such as mosaic augmentation, where four separate images are stitched together into a single frame, were heavily utilized. This forces the YOLOv8 model to recognize objects in unexpected spatial contexts, significantly reducing its reliance on standard background priors. Additionally, random photometric distortions—including brightness, contrast, and hue jittering—were introduced to synthetically simulate the rapidly changing lighting conditions typical of coastal environments. These augmentations artificially expanded the dataset size and fortified the model against overfitting.

Another critical consideration during the dataset preparation phase was mitigating class imbalance. In real-world maritime datasets, small recreational boats and coastal fishing vessels vastly outnumber massive commercial oil tankers. If trained naively, the neural network would become biased towards detecting small craft, potentially missing larger, high-risk targets. To counteract this, the training procedure incorporated focal loss, which dynamically scales the cross-entropy loss based on prediction confidence. This mathematical adjustment penalized the network more heavily for misclassifying rare, difficult examples (such as a partially occluded cargo ship) than for well-represented classes, ensuring equitable detection accuracy across all vessel profiles.

Conversely, the \gls{lstm} trajectory model was trained on the \gls{smd}. Captured via drone over the heavily trafficked Singapore Strait, the \gls{smd} provides ground-truth vessel tracks in \texttt{.mat} format. Trajectory centroids are extracted by determining the midpoint of each active bounding box:
\begin{equation}
    x_c = \frac{x_1 + x_2}{2}, \quad y_c = \frac{y_1 + y_2}{2}
    \label{eq:centroid}
\end{equation}
To ensure sufficient historical data for the \gls{lstm} input window, only trajectories spanning a minimum of 50 frames are retained. 

During preprocessing, a sliding window technique (with a stride of 1) divides the raw centroid sequences into a 50-timestep input history and a 20-timestep prediction target. To standardize the inputs, positional coordinates are normalized against the video dimensions:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{x} = \frac{x}{1920}, \quad \hat{y} = \frac{y}{1080}
    \label{eq:normalization}
\end{equation}
Per-frame velocity components are then computed as the first-order differences of these normalized positions:
\begin{equation}
    v_x^{(t)} = \hat{x}^{(t)} - \hat{x}^{(t-1)}, \quad 
    v_y^{(t)} = \hat{y}^{(t)} - \hat{y}^{(t-1)}
   \label{eq:velocity}
\end{equation}
This yields an input feature vector $\mathbf{f}^{(t)} \in \mathbb{R}^4$ at each timestep. The processed dataset is subsequently split 80:20 into training and validation sets using a fixed random seed.

\subsubsection{Evaluation Protocol}

The trajectory prediction model is evaluated using two standard metrics. The prediction performance of the proposed model is assessed using displacement-based evaluation measures that quantify the deviation between predicted and ground-truth vessel trajectories.

The \textit{Average Displacement Error} (\gls{ade}) computes the mean Euclidean distance between the predicted and actual positions across all forecasted timesteps:
\begin{equation}
    \text{ADE} = \frac{1}{K} \sum_{k=1}^{K} \sqrt{(\hat{x}_k - x_k)^2 + (\hat{y}_k - y_k)^2}
    \label{eq:LSTM1}
\end{equation}
Meanwhile, the \textit{Final Displacement Error} (\gls{fde}) assesses accuracy exclusively at the final predicted timestep:
\begin{equation}
    \text{FDE} = \sqrt{(\hat{x}_K - x_K)^2 + (\hat{y}_K - y_K)^2}
    \label{eq:LSTM2}
\end{equation}
These metrics are compared against a kinematic linear baseline to quantify the percentage improvement provided by the \gls{lstm}.

Each algorithm was selected based on its ability to address specific challenges associated with vessel detection, identity preservation, and long-horizon trajectory prediction.

\gls{yolov8} outperformed other YOLO variants primarily due to its anchor-free detection head and decoupled classification branches. In maritime environments, the aspect ratios of vessels vary wildly depending on the camera angle; an anchor-free design eliminates the need to predefine these ratios. Furthermore, its optimized feature pyramid network excels at multi-scale aggregation, allowing the system to detect massive cargo ships and tiny distant fishing boats simultaneously.

For tracking, motion-only algorithms like \gls{sort} were rejected because they rely solely on spatial overlap (\gls{iou}). In busy ports where vessels frequently cross paths or become occluded, this leads to severe identity switching. \gls{deepocsort} solves this by incorporating an \gls{osnet} appearance descriptor, meaning the tracker remembers what a ship \textit{looks like} as well as where it was heading. 

Finally, \gls{lstm} networks were chosen over Kalman filters and \glspl{gru} for trajectory prediction. While Kalman filters are computationally lightweight, they assume linear, constant-velocity motion, which fails to capture the non-linear maneuvering of real ships. Although \glspl{gru} offer a lighter neural alternative, the dedicated gating mechanisms of an \gls{lstm}—specifically its separate input, forget, and output gates—proved vastly superior at retaining the long-range temporal dependencies required to predict complex nautical turns.

\subsection{Data Fusion and Processing Pipeline}

The proposed maritime surveillance framework follows a multi-stage processing pipeline that integrates computer vision, sensor fusion, trajectory prediction, and visualization components into a unified operational workflow. Raw video streams and AIS messages are processed concurrently, allowing the system to combine visual observations with navigational telemetry. The end-to-end processing pipeline demonstrates how information flows through successive stages of detection, tracking, coordinate transformation, data fusion, trajectory prediction, and decision support.

The workflow begins with video ingestion and AIS acquisition. Video frames are processed by the YOLOv8 detector to identify vessel instances, while the DeepOCSORT tracker maintains persistent identities across consecutive frames. The tracked vessel positions are subsequently transformed from image coordinates into geographic coordinates before being associated with AIS observations. The fused vessel states are then supplied to the trajectory prediction module, which forecasts future vessel movement and supports downstream collision-risk assessment and maritime situational awareness.


The core of this pipeline is the sensor fusion strategy, which leverages the complementary strengths of both data sources. Video tracking provides rapid, frame-by-frame updates but suffers from pixel-projection noise. Conversely, \gls{ais} data offers highly precise \gls{gps} coordinates and rich metadata (such as \gls{mmsi} and vessel name) but updates sparsely. To fuse them, the system employs a nearest-neighbor association using the Haversine great-circle distance. 

When a vessel is detected on camera, its pixel position is converted to a geographic coordinate, smoothed via a Kalman filter, and compared against the live \gls{ais} registry. If an \gls{ais} record exists within a 5.5\,km radius, the visual track is enriched with the \gls{ais} metadata. If no match is found, the vessel is flagged as a \textit{dark vessel}—a potentially non-cooperative craft operating without an active transponder.

This fusion strategy significantly improves the reliability of vessel monitoring by combining the high temporal resolution of video observations with the positional accuracy and contextual metadata provided by AIS transmissions. Furthermore, the ability to identify unmatched visual detections enables the system to detect potentially non-cooperative or suspicious vessels that may otherwise remain absent from conventional AIS-based monitoring systems.


\section{Module Description}

\subsection{System Execution Flow}

The operational workflow illustrates the logical sequence of operations performed by the system, beginning with data acquisition and progressing through vessel detection, tracking, AIS fusion, trajectory prediction, and collision analysis.

The system continuously processes incoming video frames and AIS messages while evaluating a series of decision points. These include vessel detection validation, AIS association, trajectory prediction eligibility, collision-risk assessment, and alert generation. Such a structured execution workflow ensures that the surveillance pipeline operates reliably under real-time conditions.

This control logic governs when each module is invoked, which errors trigger retries or fallbacks, and when alerts are raised to the operator. Understanding this control flow is essential before examining the temporal message exchanges between individual components.

\subsection{System Interaction Sequence}

To fully grasp the complexity of the maritime surveillance platform, it is necessary to examine the strict chronological order in which data is processed. The interaction begins simultaneously at two separate endpoints: the physical camera array and the \gls{ais} receiver. On the optical side, raw video frames are pushed into a dedicated preprocessing buffer where the YOLOv8 detection module extracts bounding boxes, which are then passed to the DeepSORT tracking module for persistent identification and mathematical transformation into real-world geographic coordinates. Concurrently, the \gls{ais} receiver captures radio frequency broadcasts, extracting vessel telemetry such as MMSI, speed, heading, and GPS coordinates. Managing the temporal alignment between these two disparately timed streams—continuous video and sporadic \gls{ais}—is the primary challenge addressed by the system's interaction sequence.

The critical convergence point in this sequence occurs within the Sensor Fusion Engine, which acts as a synchronization barrier to align the optical trajectories with decoded \gls{ais} positions. Once a spatial-temporal match is established, the streams are fused into a comprehensive tensor and forwarded to the \gls{bilstm} neural network. This predictive module analyzes historical movement patterns to forecast future trajectories, which are then evaluated by the Closest Point of Approach (CPA) calculation node. If an imminent collision risk is detected, the system generates an immediate threat alert that is pushed asynchronously to the operator's visualization dashboard, completing the end-to-end processing lifecycle.

\subsection{Core Software Modules}
Rather than treating the system as a monolith, the architecture is divided into specialized, interconnected modules that handle specific operational tasks:

\subsubsection{Data Acquisition Module}
The Data Acquisition Modules manage the ingestion of raw information. The \gls{ais} receiver extracts identifiers and navigational telemetry, while the video processing module handles frame extraction and normalization for downstream computer vision tasks. 

\subsubsection{Vision and Tracking Module}
Following ingestion, the Vision and Tracking Modules take over. \gls{yolov8} generates bounding boxes around all visible ships, and \gls{deepocsort} assigns persistent identities to these detections, ensuring continuity even if a vessel briefly passes behind an obstruction.

\subsubsection{Transformation and Fusion Module}
The data is then contextualized by the Transformation and Fusion Modules. Image-space coordinates are mathematically projected into real-world geographic coordinates. This allows the data fusion engine to pair the visual observations with the corresponding \gls{ais} telemetry, resulting in a highly reliable, metadata-enriched vessel track.

\subsubsection{Analytical and Visualization Module}
Finally, the Analytical Modules generate actionable intelligence. The trajectory predictor uses a \gls{bilstm} network to forecast future movements based on historical patterns, while the collision detection system analyzes these forecasts to flag potential intersecting paths. All of this information is synthesized by the visualization module, which renders the bounding boxes, predicted paths, and alert notifications onto an intuitive operator dashboard.

The dashboard is designed with a focus on cognitive ergonomics, ensuring that operators are not overwhelmed by raw telemetry. Instead, the interface employs a priority-based coloring scheme where standard vessels are outlined in green, whereas vessels involved in a high-risk CPA event are highlighted in flashing red. This visual hierarchy is augmented by a tabular sidebar that lists exact coordinates, calculated speeds, and the estimated time until potential impact. By aggregating these critical metrics into a single unified view, the visualization module effectively bridges the gap between complex backend analytics and rapid, human-in-the-loop decision-making.

\section{Summary}
This chapter detailed the foundational design, mathematical methodology, and architectural structure of the proposed maritime surveillance system. Operational parameters were established for the entire pipeline, from the asynchronous ingestion of coastal video streams and \gls{ais} telemetry to the final output generation. The selection of state-of-the-art algorithms, specifically YOLOv8 for object detection and DeepSORT for robust temporal tracking, was thoroughly justified against the unique challenges of the maritime domain. 

Furthermore, the integration of a \gls{bilstm} neural network for trajectory prediction and a deterministic Closest Point of Approach (CPA) algorithm for collision risk assessment was explored in depth. By establishing a rigid, modular sequence—where spatial transformations seamlessly feed into the sensor fusion engine—the architecture guarantees real-time responsiveness without sacrificing analytical accuracy. Ultimately, by routing complex multimodal data through this structured sequence of functional modules, the system offers a cohesive, scalable, and highly intelligent solution for modern vessel monitoring and port security.
